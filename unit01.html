  • 1.4
    Describe the risks that might impact digital projects

    Project risks are uncertainties that expose a project to potential failure to achieve its goals. Identifying, evaluating and treating risk is an ongoing project management activity that seeks to improve project results by avoiding, reducing or transferring risks. Project risk management also provides stakeholders with the visibility and clarifies accountability for any accepted risks. The following types of risks are commonly encountered by projects:

    • Scope creep - uncontrolled change to a projects scope
    • Project definition – not understanding dependencies or assumptions
    • Budget – failure to successfully estimate budget or cost overruns
    • Resistance to change – defending the status quo and resisting the change
    • Integration – underestimating the risk of change to technology, information and process
    • Resource/Skills – failing to secure sufficient skilled workers
    • 3rd party – failure of a 3rd party to deliver contractual obligations
    • Sponsor support – failure of the sponsor(s) to support the funding and change
    • Operational risk – potential service outages and changes that disrupt the project delivery
    • Benefit shortfall – not meeting the business case benefit specifics in the business case

  • 1.5
    Describe how you would go about selecting and using IT systems and software

    Selecting the correct software for a business can be one of the most important decisions to make that would feed the growth and profits for several years to come. Regardless of the size of your business a well thought-out plan will focus software decisions and help to make choices that will serve a business well in the future.

    The following steps are key to the overall selection:

    • Specifying requirements – define the requirements and identify key stakeholders, team members and subject matter experts.
    • Identify vendors – review the market place, research the internet, talk to vendor communities, create an outline RFP that meets the requirements and invite a vendor shortlist to participate further.
    • Attend vendor definition, review and demonstration sessions to confirm requirement, design and overall conformity. Extend the definition of the RFP where required and iteratively review it until all requirements are met with all details finalised.
    • Reference site visits – visit or communication with reference sites for each of the vendors to learn valuable lessons from their experiences (both vendor and product).
    • Make a final decision - this should be done with reviewing all of the previous information where it should be clear which system to purchase.
    • Before progressions to on-boarding, commence with final contract negations with regards to service levels and formal obligation.

  • 1.7
    Describe legal guidelines and constraints that impact digital projects.

    Introduced into effect in the European Union (EU) in May 2018 GDPR (General Data Protection Regulation) has kick-started a wave of global privacy laws that have forever changed how we use the internet (and marketing over the internet). The selling of personal data to advertisers can be very profitable indeed (as demonstrated by global companies such as Google or Facebook), GDPR has therefore been introduced to tell companies of all size how they can and can't use your information. The following are two of the most common GDPR terms today and understanding them is a vital part of becoming familiar with data protection in general:

    • Privacy by design - when creating products (websites for example) data collection must be kept to a minimum and sufficient granular security measures must be introduced to ensure adequate protection in all places at all times.
    • Consent - obtaining consent simply means asking permission data, collection practices must be written in a clear and simple language and must be explicitly agreed to.

    Under GDPR, methods that digital projects use to obtain consent are held to a much higher standard, this includes:

    • Active opt-in – Consent will require a positive opt-in through clear affirmative action.
    • Active opt-out – Individuals can withdraw consent as simply as opt-in.
    • Data use – Individuals should get as much detail as possible about what their data will be processed for.
    • Granular - Consent (or withdrawing consent) is to be available for each distinct processing activity.
    • Unbundling – Consent requests must be separated from terms and conditions.
    • Not a pre-condition of service - Consent should not be a precondition of service usage and/or signup.
    • Named – It should be clear to the individual who will be processing their data including any third-parties.
    • Right of access - Individuals have a right to access any data you hold on them (when, how and to what they consented to as well as the data that is held).
    • Minimal collection and processing - marketers are only permitted to process the data that they really need and must have a legal justification for doing so.
